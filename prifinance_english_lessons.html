Lesson 1 — Grammar Patterns for Sales
Three grammar structures you use on every call — with real Prifinance examples
Vocabulary — 10 key terms
jurisdiction
A country or region where a company is registered and follows local law.
"We work with 30+ jurisdictions — Europe, Asia, offshore."
tax rate
The percentage of profit paid as tax.
"The tax rate in Cyprus is 12.5% — one of the lowest in the EU."
dividend
Money paid to company owners from the profit.
"In Estonia, you only pay tax when you take out a dividend."
nominee
A person who appears in official documents but has no real control.
"Your name appears nowhere — we use a nominee director."
compliance
Following all legal rules required by banks and regulators.
"Banks in Cyprus have strict compliance — they need a lot of documents."
beneficial owner
The real person who owns and controls the company.
"You are the beneficial owner — the nominee has no real power."
incorporation
The process of officially registering a company.
"Incorporation in Estonia takes 3–5 business days."
EMI
Electronic Money Institution — like a bank, but easier to open. Does SEPA, SWIFT, multi-currency.
"We open an EMI account remotely — no visit needed."
substance
Real physical presence in a country — office, employees, local director.
"Local banks require substance — a real connection to the country."
territorial taxation
A tax system where only income earned inside the country is taxed.
"Hong Kong uses territorial taxation — income from outside is not taxed."
Grammar — Pattern 1: Present Simple

When to use: Facts, tax rates, rules, how things work. You use this every time you explain a jurisdiction to a client.

STRUCTURE: Subject + verb (base form)
Use for permanent facts and standard conditions.
  • The tax rate in Cyprus is 12.5%.
  • Hong Kong uses territorial taxation.
  • Registration takes 3–5 business days.
  • The registry in Estonia is public — anyone can look it up.
  • We work with clients in 30+ jurisdictions.
  • A nominee director appears in documents but has no real control.
Grammar — Pattern 2: Present Perfect

When to use: Your experience, results, what you have done. Use this to build trust with the client.

STRUCTURE: have/has + past participle
Use for experience and completed results that are still relevant now.
  • We have registered over 11,000 companies worldwide.
  • We have worked with clients from the UAE, Pakistan, and France.
  • Have you ever used an offshore structure before?
  • I have already sent you the quote — check your email.
  • Our clients have come back for their second and third company.
Grammar — Pattern 3: First Conditional

When to use: Proposals, timelines, recommendations. Your main tool for creating urgency and making offers.

STRUCTURE: If + present simple, will/can + verb
Use for real, likely situations — offers and consequences.
  • If you register now, you can start in 2 weeks.
  • If privacy is important, we can arrange a nominee director.
  • If you have a deadline, we should move quickly.
  • If you need CNY, I will recommend Chouzhou Bank.
  • If the registry is public, we can keep your name private with a nominee.

Lesson 2 — Opening & Qualification
How to start a call and ask the right questions — the most important part of any call
Vocabulary — 8 key phrases
to qualify a client
To ask questions to understand what the client really needs before making a recommendation.
"I need 2 minutes to qualify you — then I give you the right setup."
inbound
The client contacted you first.
"Thanks for reaching out — happy to help."
cold call
You call the client first — they didn't expect it.
"I noticed you work internationally and thought it might be relevant."
to mirror
To repeat back what the client said — shows you listened.
"So you're doing IT consulting — clients in Europe. Got it."
nominee service
We provide a local director/shareholder so the client's name is not public.
"We handle that with a nominee service — your name appears nowhere."
proof of address
A document showing where you live — utility bill or bank statement.
"We need a passport and proof of address to start."
to relocate
To move yourself or your business to another country.
"Are you also thinking about relocating — or is this just for the company?"
lead time
The time needed before a deadline — registration, banking, etc.
"Registration takes 2 weeks — we need to factor in that lead time."
Opening — Cold call

Goal: Build trust and get permission to ask questions. Don't pitch the product — pitch the conversation.

Step by step
  • "Hi [Name], this is [Your Name] from Prifinance."
    Name + company. No "is it convenient?" — it gives them an easy exit.
  • "We help international businesses register companies and open bank accounts — Europe, Asia, offshore. 28 years in the market, 11,000+ clients."
    One sentence. Keep it short.
  • "I noticed [you work internationally / you're expanding] and thought it might be relevant."
    Why you're calling. Make it about them, not you.
  • "Before I walk you through anything — can I ask you a couple of quick questions? It'll make sure I give you the right setup, not a generic one."
    Ask for permission. This works very well — it shows respect and creates trust.
If client immediately asks for price
  • "Absolutely — I just need 2 minutes to understand your setup, otherwise I'll quote you the wrong package. It'll be worth it."
    Never give price before qualifying. Polite but firm.
Opening — Inbound
When the client contacted you first
  • "Hi [Name], thanks for reaching out to Prifinance."
  • "I'm [Your Name] — happy to help you find the right solution."
  • "To make sure I point you in the best direction, could I ask a couple of quick questions about what you're looking to set up?"
Qualification — 5 questions

Important: This is not an interview. After each answer — react. Show you understood. Then ask the next question.

Q1 — Business and activity
"What type of business are you running — or planning to run? And where are your main clients or partners located?"

Why: Determines the right jurisdiction and contract structure.
React: "So you're doing [X] — clients in [Y]. Got it. That's exactly the setup we work with regularly."
Q2 — Privacy
"Is privacy important to you — do you mind if your name appears publicly as director and shareholder?"

Why: In most jurisdictions (HK, UK, EU) the registry is public. If privacy matters — nominee service is needed. Changes the price.
React (if yes): "No problem — we handle that with a nominee director and shareholder. Your name appears nowhere publicly. You stay in full control through a Trust Declaration and Power of Attorney."
Q3 — Banking
"Do you need a bank account as well? And what currencies do you mainly work in — USD, EUR, local currency?"

Why: Banking is usually the client's main pain point. Currency determines which bank to recommend.
React: "Good to know — that actually changes which banking option I'd recommend for you."
Q4 — Remote or visit?
"Are you comfortable with a short trip — say 1–2 days — if needed for the bank? Or does everything need to be fully remote?"

Why: Chouzhou Bank (China) requires a 1–2 day visit. If remote only — EMI or online bank.
React (if remote): "No problem — we have two fully remote options that work just as well."
Q5 — Timeline
"Is there a specific timeline you're working towards — a supplier contract, a payment coming in, a launch date?"

Why: Urgency is your main tool for closing. A deadline turns "I'll think about it" into action.
React (if yes): "Then we should move quickly — registration takes 2–3 weeks once we start. If your [meeting / contract] is in [X], we're actually cutting it close."
Transition to presentation
"Great — that gives me exactly what I need. Based on what you've told me, let me walk you through the setup that makes the most sense for you. I'll cover the tax structure, the company, banking, and then we'll look at the numbers."

Lesson 3 — Objections & Closing
How to handle "no" and turn it into "yes" — and how to close every call with a clear next step
Vocabulary — 8 key phrases
to stage it
To split a purchase into steps — company first, bank account later.
"Let's stage it — start with just the company, bank is a separate step."
to lean towards
To prefer or recommend something specific.
"Based on what you told me, I'd lean towards Estonia. Here's why."
to factor in
To include something in your planning.
"We need to factor in the 2-week registration time."
onboarding form
The document we send to start the process.
"I can send you the onboarding form right now. Shall we do that?"
to reconnect
To speak again after a break.
"When's a good time to reconnect — say, next week?"
worth keeping in mind
Something important to remember.
"One thing worth keeping in mind — registration takes 2 weeks."
formal quote
A written document with the full price breakdown.
"Let me send you a formal quote — takes me about an hour."
to summarise
To repeat the key points before closing.
"So to summarise: Estonia company, EMI account, total around €3,300."
Closing — 3 types

Rule: Every call ends with a clear next step. No next step = lost deal.

Always summarise before closing
"So to summarise: you'd register [company type] in [country] — [remote / requires visit], takes [timeline]. [Tax benefit] because of [reason]. For banking, we go with [option] — ready in [timeline]. Total to be fully operational: around [price]."

This shows you listened. It makes the decision feel simple.
Soft close — client is warm but not ready to pay now
"Let me send you a formal quote with the full breakdown — takes me about an hour. What's the best email for you?"

Low pressure. You get the email. You stay in contact.
Hard close — client is ready
"Great — to move forward I just need your passport copy, proof of address, and three company name options. I can send you the onboarding form right now. Shall we do that?"

Clear and direct. "Shall we do that?" is a simple yes/no — not aggressive, just confident.
Not ready — keep the relationship
"No problem. I'll send you a summary of everything we discussed. When's a good time to reconnect — say, in a week?"

Always get a specific date for the next call. "I'll think about it" without a date = lost lead.
Objection Handling

Remember: Most objections are not "no" — they are "I'm not sure yet." Your job is to find what's missing and fill that gap.

"It's too expensive."
▼
"Let's stage it. Start with just the company — €1,400. You're registered, you can sign contracts and issue invoices. Bank account is a separate step.

If you go with the remote EMI option, total is around €3,300 — and that's everything you need to be fully operational. You don't need to do it all at once."
Key move: split the cost into stages. Make it feel smaller and more manageable.
"I need to think about it."
▼
"Of course — take the time you need.

One thing worth keeping in mind: registration takes 2–3 weeks once we start. Do you have any supplier meetings or contracts coming up where you'll need the company ready?

If there's a date, we need to factor in that lead time."
Key move: find a deadline. A real deadline turns "I'll think" into action.
"I'm comparing you with other providers."
▼
"That makes sense — you should compare.

A few things worth checking with any provider: Do they use nominees from the actual jurisdiction? Do they have direct banking relationships, or just introductions? And what happens if something goes wrong after setup?

We've been in this since 1997. Our clients come back for the second and third company. That's the benchmark I'd suggest."
Key move: give them specific questions to ask competitors — questions you know you win on.
"Can I do it myself / cheaper?"
▼
"You can — the registry process is technically public.

But what people don't anticipate: the nominee arrangements, banking compliance, apostille chains, and ongoing filings. We've done this hundreds of times across 30+ jurisdictions.

The difference is we know what banks actually accept and what gets applications rejected — and we handle the parts that get stuck."
Key move: acknowledge it's possible, then show the hidden complexity they haven't thought about.
"I'm not sure which jurisdiction is right."
▼
"That's exactly why I'm here — let's figure it out together.

It comes down to three things: where your clients are, where you want to bank, and how much tax efficiency matters to you.

Based on what you told me — [repeat facts from qualification] — I'd lean towards [jurisdiction]. Here's why..."
Key move: never say "all options are equally good." Make a specific recommendation — this builds trust.

Lesson 4 — Presentation
How to present the right solution after qualification — always in the same order: Tax → Structure → Banking → Accounting
Vocabulary — 8 key terms
undistributed profit
Money that stays inside the company and is not paid out to owners.
"In Estonia, undistributed profit is taxed at 0%."
corporate income tax (CIT)
Tax paid by the company on its profit.
"Cyprus has a CIT of 12.5% — one of the lowest in the EU."
withholding tax
Tax deducted at source when paying dividends or interest to foreign owners.
"BVI has 0% withholding tax — you keep everything."
registered address
The official legal address of the company in the country of registration.
"A registered address is mandatory — we provide it as part of the package."
power of attorney (POA)
A legal document giving someone authority to act on your behalf.
"The nominee signs a power of attorney — you stay in full control."
trust declaration
A document confirming that the nominee holds shares on behalf of the real owner.
"The trust declaration protects your ownership rights legally."
annual filing
The yearly report a company must submit to the tax authority.
"Annual filing is required — we handle all of it for you."
fully operational
Ready to do business — company registered, account open, everything in place.
"Total to be fully operational: around €3,600."
Transition — from qualification to presentation

Rule: Never present before you qualify. And always start the presentation by referring back to what the client told you — this shows you listened.

Opening line
"Great — that gives me exactly what I need. Based on what you've told me, let me walk you through the setup that makes the most sense for you. I'll cover the tax structure, how the company is set up, banking, and then we'll look at the numbers."

Never present more than 1–2 options. Pick the best fit from qualification. Too many options = confusion = no decision.
Block 1 — Tax (always start here)

Why first: This is where the client gets excited. Tax savings are the main emotional trigger. Don't rush — let the numbers land.

Opening phrase
"Here's the key thing about [jurisdiction] — and it's what makes it so powerful for your specific situation."

Then explain the tax advantage tied directly to their business model. Always add:

"This isn't a loophole or a grey area — it's written into [country] law. Thousands of international companies use exactly this structure, and it's fully compliant."
Tax cheat sheet — key phrases per jurisdiction
Estonia: "You pay 0% corporate tax as long as money stays in the company. You only pay 22% when you actually take out dividends. So if you're growing and reinvesting — you pay nothing during that period."

Cyprus: "Corporate tax is 12.5% — one of the lowest in the EU. And when you pay dividends to yourselves as non-residents — that's 0%. So you pay tax once at company level, and that's it."

Hong Kong: "Hong Kong uses territorial taxation — only income sourced inside Hong Kong is taxed. Since your clients are outside HK, your income is not taxed at all. That's 0% on your trading profit."

BVI: "BVI companies are 100% tax-free — no corporate tax, no capital gains tax, no withholding tax. Everything stays with you."

Hungary: "Hungary has the lowest corporate tax in the EU — 9%. Simple, clean, and fully compliant."
Block 2 — Company structure
Standard explanation
"The entity you'd register is [company type] — the standard [country] structure. Globally recognised, straightforward to set up."

If the registry is public and client wants privacy:
"The registry is public — so anyone can look up who the director and shareholder are. Based on what you told me about privacy, we'd set up a nominee director and shareholder. Your name appears nowhere publicly. You stay in full control through a Trust Declaration and Power of Attorney. The nominee has zero real influence on any decision."

If nominee is required by law (not optional):
"In [country], a local director is required by law — not just for privacy, it's a legal requirement. We provide that as part of the package."
Block 3 — Banking

Rule: Don't list all options. Recommend one specific option based on their answers in qualification. Then mention alternatives briefly.

Recommendation phrase
"Based on what you told me — [reference their answer] — I'd recommend starting with [option]. Here's why: [reason tied to their situation]. The other options are available too, but this is the one that fits your setup best right now."
Quick banking guide
Client needs CNY, willing to travel: "Chouzhou Bank in China — USD, EUR, CNY, RUB. Requires a 1–2 day visit."

Everything remote, HK company: "HK online bank — video verification, ready in about 2 weeks."

Needs EUR IBAN, remote: "EMI account — €1,900, fully remote, 1–2 weeks."

EU company, traditional bank: "We can assist with a local bank — timeline is longer and compliance is stricter, but it's a full banking relationship."

High-risk business (dating, crypto, etc.): "Traditional banks are difficult for your type of business. EMI is the realistic and reliable option — it covers everything you need operationally."
Block 4 — Accounting (end on good news)
How to present accounting
"One more thing worth mentioning — accounting."

Estonia / Cyprus / UK: "Annual filing is required, but we handle all of it. Monthly accounting starts at [price]. Nothing you need to set up from day one — we'll guide you when the time comes."

HK: "Hong Kong requires annual reporting and an audit. But the first audit is only required 18 months after registration. So you register, open your account, start trading — and we sort the books when the time comes. You only pay for the company and the bank account upfront."

This block always ends on a positive note — accounting is not a burden, it's handled.
