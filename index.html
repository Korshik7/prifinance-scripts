Estonia → Alternative pitch
Full client conversation

B2 English · Real use + language learning · IT/Consulting profile
You (Sales)
Client
Sales note / tip
Phase 1 — Opening & rapport
Situation: Client reached out via website or referral. Said they want to open a company in Estonia. First call, ~30–40 min. Your goal: understand their real need before presenting anything.
You Hey [Name], thanks for reaching out. I saw your message — you're looking at Estonia, right? Before we get into the details, I'd love to get a better picture of what you're working on. Do you have a few minutes?
Client Yeah sure, I've got time. Basically I run a small IT consultancy, mostly working with clients in Germany and the Netherlands. Someone told me Estonia is good for taxes, so I wanted to look into it.
You Got it. And are you currently operating through a company somewhere, or are you working as an individual right now?
Client Individual, yeah. I'm based in Ukraine, so everything just goes to my personal account. It's getting complicated.
You Understood. And when you say complicated — is it more about the tax side, or about how your clients pay you, or both?
Client Honestly both. A couple of German clients asked for invoices from a proper company. And I'm tired of paying high personal income tax on everything.
Note Two clear pain points identified: invoicing/legitimacy + tax optimization. Now you can position Estonia directly against both.
Phase 2 — Qualifying questions
You Makes sense. A few more questions so I can point you in the right direction. What's your rough monthly revenue — just a ballpark is fine?
Client Around €8,000–12,000 a month. Depends on the projects.
You Good. And are you planning to reinvest most of that back into the business, or do you need to pull it out as personal income regularly?
Client I need some for living, but a big chunk stays in the business. I'm trying to grow it, maybe hire someone next year.
You Perfect, that's actually very relevant. One more — do you have any plans to relocate, or are you fine staying where you are and just having a foreign company?
Client No plans to move right now. I just want a clean structure for the business.
Note Profile confirmed: IT services, EU clients, high reinvestment, no relocation. Estonia is the primary fit. Slovakia as backup if he wants a local bank account.
Phase 3 — Estonia presentation
You Alright, so based on what you told me — Estonia makes a lot of sense for your situation, and I'll explain exactly why.
You The company type is called OÜ — think of it as a standard limited company. One of the biggest advantages is the tax model. In Estonia, you only pay corporate tax when you distribute dividends. As long as the profit stays inside the company — zero tax. Nothing.
Client Wait, so if I leave money in the company I pay nothing?
You Exactly. You can accumulate, reinvest, pay your suppliers — all of that is tax-free. The moment you decide to take out dividends, that's when the 22% kicks in. But if you're growing and reinvesting — that money works for you, not for the tax office.
Client That sounds almost too good. Is there a catch?
You Good question — and I'd rather be upfront about the nuances than oversell it. The one thing to keep in mind is substance. Estonia is not an offshore. If the tax authority in your home country looks at your situation, they want to see that the company has real business activity — invoices going out, a bank account, accounting in order. Which for someone in your position, it's all very natural. You're doing real work, right?
Client Yeah, of course. I have real contracts with real clients.
You Then you're in good shape. Now, registration itself — it's fully remote. We handle everything through a power of attorney, so you don't need to fly to Estonia. The whole process takes about 3 to 5 business days once we have your documents.
You And for the bank account — this is where a lot of people get confused. Local Estonian banks like LHV or SEB are very selective. They want to see real ties to Estonia, which most of our clients don't have. So what we do instead is set up an account with Montify — it's our main banking partner, FCA-licensed in the UK. You get a real IBAN, SEPA transfers, multi-currency. Your German clients can pay you just like they would any other European company.
Client And that's accepted? Like a normal bank?
You For 90% of B2B operations — yes, completely. It's not a traditional bank in the sense that it doesn't give out loans, but for sending and receiving payments, invoicing, payroll — it works exactly the same. We actually use Montify ourselves as a company, which tells you something.
Note "We use it ourselves" — social proof that doesn't feel forced. Use it naturally when it fits.
Phase 4 — Handling objection + pricing
Client Okay. And what does all of this cost?
You So let me break it down for you. The company registration is €900. Because we're doing it remotely via power of attorney, that adds €500 — covers the notary and translations. Then the legal address is €375 per month. And the Montify account is a one-time €1,900.
You On top of that, accounting is €110 a month — that includes your monthly reporting and we charge one extra month for the annual report. So your initial investment to get everything up and running is roughly €3,800, and then around €485 a month ongoing.
Client Hmm. That's more than I expected honestly.
You I hear you. Let me put it in context though. You mentioned you're earning around €10k a month. If even 60% of that stays in the company as profit — that's €6,000 that in most jurisdictions would be taxed at 20–30%. In Estonia, that's zero until you choose to take it out. Over a year, we're talking about €15,000 to €20,000 in deferred tax. The structure pays for itself in the first two months.
Client Yeah, when you put it that way… okay. And what about VAT? My German clients sometimes ask about it.
You Good that you bring that up. If you're providing B2B services to clients in Germany — meaning they're companies, not individuals — the reverse charge mechanism applies. You don't charge VAT, they handle it on their end. So for your current setup, you likely don't need a VAT number right away. But if at some point you start working with end consumers or need it for platform compliance, we can take care of that for €600.
Phase 5 — Alternative: Slovakia
When to offer Slovakia: Client wants a traditional bank account, needs EU credibility with local banking, or is considering relocation to the EU.
You Actually, since you mentioned your clients are asking for proper invoices — let me also walk you through one alternative that might be worth considering. It's Slovakia.
Client Slovakia? What's different there?
You So the main difference is the banking. With Slovakia, we can open an account in a real local bank — PKO Bank — and we can do it remotely, without you flying there. That's actually quite rare in Europe. Most local banks require a personal visit.
You Now, the tax model is different — it's a flat 21% on profit, so not as aggressive as Estonia. But some clients prefer having a traditional bank behind their company name. Especially if they're dealing with larger corporate clients who do strict due diligence.
Client And there's no tax advantage like Estonia?
You Correct, the tax model is more standard. But Slovakia has one more thing going for it — if you ever decide to relocate to the EU, we can apply for a business residence permit through the Slovakian consulate in your country. You don't even need to travel there for the first stage. So it's a two-in-one: a solid EU company today, and a potential path to EU residency if you need it later.
Client Interesting. I'm not planning to move now but that's good to know.
You Exactly. The way I see it — if your priority right now is tax efficiency and you're fine with an EMI account, Estonia is the cleaner, cheaper option. If you want a real bank account from day one and you're willing to pay a bit more, Slovakia makes more sense. Both are fully remote, both are EU.
Note Always give the client a clear framework to decide. Don't leave it open-ended — guide them to the choice that fits their stated priority.
Phase 6 — Closing
You So based on everything you've told me — IT services, EU clients, reinvesting most of your profit — I'd lean towards Estonia as the primary recommendation. Does that align with what you had in mind?
Client Yeah, Estonia feels right. What are the next steps?
You Great. I'll send over a detailed proposal by end of day — it'll include the full breakdown, timeline, and what documents we need from you. Basically just a passport copy and proof of address to get started. Once you confirm, we can get the ball rolling within 24 hours.
Client Sounds good. Looking forward to it.
You Perfect. I'll also include a short note on the accounting requirements so you know exactly what to expect. Talk soon.
